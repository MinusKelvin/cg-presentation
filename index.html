How Computer Graphics Work
An Interactive Presentation
Start
Your screen is a grid of pixels, and so all computer graphics must be put of a grid of pixels somehow.

The screen is basically a coordinate system, and so locations are just a pair of coordinates.
Using whole-number coordinates, points fall exactly on pixels' centers and everything is really easy.

Drawing lines is simple, you just start at the beginning of the line and step along it until you reach the end of the line.
Drawing filled shapes, always triangles, is a little bit harder, but is still relatively simple to do quickly and efficently.

The only filled shapes we draw are triangles because all other shapes can be made (or approximated) with triangles, and it simplifies things a ton.
We can rotate these around the center of the screen with a little bit of math and...
Uh oh! That rotation is really choppy and not very smooth. Rather ugly.

The problem here is that since we're using whole number coordinates, we're losing information about the exact location of the triangle's corners.
Ah! Much better. By changing our coordinate system from using whole numbers to using decimals, we can target parts of pixels instead of just the centers of them.

This makes drawing stuff more complicated, but looks so much nicer.
One color is kinda boring. Here's 3 colors!

We colored one point of the triangle red, another green, and the last blue. The colors are smoothly shifted from one to another inside the triangle.

Now that we know how graphics end up on the screen, it's time for something more interesting: 3D

This surface is generated from several sine waves based on each points' position and time. It's made of 20,000 triangles.
You can see the current time value at the top. It's not changing, so the surface is not changing.

Now that time is changing, the surface is changing. You can use the above slider to change the timescale.
As the surface changes, the lighting on the surface also changes. Lighting is quite interesting.

It requires two things at a minimum: a light direction, and a surface normal.
A surface normal is the direction that sticks straight of out a surface, or in other words the direction at a right angle to a surface.

I'm showing those as blue lines here.
The light direction is the direction from the light source, which in this case is the same for every point to make it look like sunlight.

You can use the slider at the bottom to change the light's direction.
Once we have the surface normal and the light direction, we can easily calculate how bright the surface should be.

Oh, and all that lighting stuff is run per-pixel, which works out to your computer being really, really fast.
